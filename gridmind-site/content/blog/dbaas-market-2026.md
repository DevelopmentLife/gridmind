The database-as-a-service market hit an estimated $28 billion in 2025 and is showing no signs of slowing down. The big story isn't raw growth though — it's consolidation. After years of fragmentation across dozens of managed database providers, enterprise buyers are collapsing their vendor sprawl into two or three platforms. AWS Aurora, Google AlloyDB, and a handful of purpose-built AI-native databases are capturing the lion's share of new workloads, while mid-tier players scramble to differentiate on price or specialization.

What's driving this shift is the rise of vector and hybrid workloads. As teams embed AI into their applications, the requirement to run both OLTP and vector similarity search on the same infrastructure — without managing two separate systems — has become a genuine purchasing criterion. The managed databases that can't answer "yes" to that question cleanly are losing deals at an accelerating rate. This isn't theoretical: in Q4 2025, three of the top five cloud providers reported vector-capable managed database deployments growing at over 200% year-on-year.

The practical implication for engineering teams is that database operations are becoming harder, not easier. More data types, more query patterns, more compliance requirements — all on infrastructure that is increasingly abstracted away. The teams winning right now are the ones who have automated the operational layer entirely, freeing engineers to focus on schema design and query optimization rather than patching, failover, and capacity planning. That's the gap autonomous database operations platforms are built to close.
